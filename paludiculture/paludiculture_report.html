Section 01

What is Paludiculture?

Paludiculture refers to wet agriculture or forestry practiced on peatlands — either naturally wet or actively rewetted peat soils. It involves cultivating plant species adapted to waterlogged conditions while keeping the peat wet year-round, which prevents peat oxidation, minimises CO2 emissions, and halts land subsidence caused by drainage-based farming.

Unlike conventional drainage agriculture, paludiculture harvests only above-ground biomass. Below-ground roots and organic matter are left intact, contributing to peat preservation or formation. The harvested biomass — from reeds, willows, sedges, or shrubs — feeds into construction, insulation, bioenergy, and advanced materials like mycelium-based composites (MBCs).

Paludiculture is currently being piloted and expanded across Germany, the Netherlands, and other European countries with significant drained peatland areas. It represents a practical land-use strategy that simultaneously delivers material production and critical ecosystem services.

Section 02

Key Principles & Benefits

Paludiculture is built on five interconnected principles that distinguish it from conventional agriculture and make it compatible with EU climate and biodiversity targets.

💧
Peatland rewetting
Water tables are raised to near-surface levels year-round, halting peat oxidation — a major GHG source when peatlands are drained for agriculture.
🌿
Biomass production
Above-ground biomass is harvested periodically while below-ground material is preserved, contributing to soil carbon and peat formation over time.
🔄
Circular bio-economy
Harvested biomass is used for construction, insulation, fibres, bioenergy, bioplastics, and MBCs — fitting fully into circular material flows.
🌿
Ecosystem services
Rewetted peatlands support biodiversity, water purification, nutrient retention, and flood buffering — functions lost under drainage farming.
⚖️
Sustainable land use
Offers a productive alternative to drainage-based agriculture on degraded peatlands, reducing land-use conflict and climate impact simultaneously.
Section 03

From Paludiculture to MBC

Mycelium-based composites (MBCs) use fungal mycelium from species like Pleurotus, Trametes, or Ganoderma as a natural binder for lignocellulosic substrates. Paludiculture shrubs provide an ideal renewable, wetland-sourced feedstock for this process.

02

03

Harvest stems every 2-4 years; roots stay intact

05

Mycelium growth
Inoculate with fungal spawn; incubate 10-25 days
06
🧱
Dry & press
Heat-inactivate; press or mould into final composite
Section 04

Wetland Shrub Profiles

The following species are paludiculture-compatible shrubs with lignocellulosic biomass suitable as MBC substrates. Each profile covers ecology, paludiculture role, MBC suitability, and practical considerations. Image placeholders are included for each species.

Species 01
Salix spp.
Willow · Weide (German) · Shrub Willows
Waterlogged Short-rotation coppice MBC substrate Europe

Fast-growing deciduous shrubs and small trees (e.g. S. viminalis, basket willow). Thrive in wet and rewetted peatland conditions. Widely used across European paludiculture pilot sites as a primary biomass crop in short-rotation coppice (SRC) systems, harvested every 2-4 years.

Paludiculture role
  • Primary SRC biomass species; high above-ground yield per hectare
  • Planted as buffer strips along rewetted ditches and margins
  • Rapid regrowth after coppicing; low management input after establishment
  • Root systems stabilise wet soils and contribute to peat preservation
  • Nutrient uptake from waterlogged soils improves water quality
MBC suitability
  • Rich in cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin -- ideal lignocellulosic substrate
  • Analogous to birch chips and wood sawdust used successfully with Trametes and Pleurotus
  • Chips and shreds bind well under mycelial colonisation; yields strong, mouldable panels
  • Tunable particle size gives control over final composite density and porosity
Considerations

Bark content may affect colonisation uniformity. Blending with straw or other fibrous substrates often optimises the nutrient balance for mycelial penetration.

MBC potential
Excellent
Species 02
Frangula alnus
Alder Buckthorn · Faulbaum (German)
Fen / peatland MBC substrate Europe

A deciduous wetland shrub common in European fens, wet heathlands, and peatland margins. Produces woody stems with bark containing medicinally relevant compounds. Provides renewable biomass from rewetted sites with minimal management input and strong ecological value.

Paludiculture role
  • Naturally colonises rewetted fen and peatland edges
  • Low-maintenance biomass from marginal wet areas unsuited to willows
  • Supports insect biodiversity and wetland habitat structure
  • Bark compounds have medicinal and raw material uses
MBC suitability
  • Woody bark and stems provide lignocellulosic fibre for substrate blends
  • Comparable to other woody shrubs used as MBC substrates
  • Suitable for panel and foam applications when chipped
  • Adds substrate diversity to multi-species blends
Considerations

Lower volume yield than willows. Fungal compatibility testing recommended before large-scale use. Best employed in blended substrate mixes.

MBC potential
Good
Species 03
Myrica gale
Bog Myrtle · Sweet Gale · Gagel (German)
Bog / wet heath Blend substrate Europe / N. America

An aromatic deciduous shrub native to bogs and wet heathlands. Tolerates highly acidic, waterlogged peat. Leaves, catkins, and fruits contain waxy aromatic compounds with natural antimicrobial properties. A niche but ecologically valuable paludiculture species with specialty application potential in MBCs.

Paludiculture role
  • Thrives in highly acidic, waterlogged peat conditions
  • Nitrogen-fixing root nodules enrich surrounding soil
  • Leaves and fruits used in food, medicine, and natural dyes
  • Ecological and habitat value on rewetted bog sites
MBC suitability
  • Stems and leaves provide lignocellulosic substrate material
  • Aromatic essential oils may confer antimicrobial properties during colonisation
  • Best used in blends rather than as a pure substrate
  • Potential for bioactive or scented specialty composite products
Considerations

Essential oils may inhibit some fungal species. Preliminary compatibility tests with target fungi are strongly advised before use at any scale.

MBC potential
Moderate (blends)
Species 04
Cephalanthus occidentalis
Buttonbush · Common Buttonbush
Flooded wetland MBC substrate North America

A multi-stemmed deciduous shrub native to North American wetlands, tolerating prolonged flooding and waterlogged soils. Produces distinctive spherical white flower heads. Valuable for wetland habitat in riparian and paludiculture contexts, and suitable as a regional biomass species for North American MBC supply chains.

Paludiculture role
  • Tolerates sustained flooding; suitable for rewetted wetland systems
  • Fibrous root systems aid stabilisation of wet and riparian soils
  • High wildlife value: pollinators, waterfowl, and wetland birds
  • Potential for integrated riparian biomass and habitat systems
MBC suitability
  • Woody stems provide lignocellulosic substrate comparable to other shrubs
  • Suitable for structural composite applications when chipped
  • Extreme wetland tolerance offers reliability in fully flooded production systems
  • Regional availability makes it practical for North American operations
Considerations

Limited to North American contexts. Lower research base for MBC applications compared to willow -- further substrate trials needed before commercial adoption.

MBC potential
Good (regional)

Section 06

MBC Production Tips

Practical guidance for processing paludiculture shrub biomass into mycelium composite substrates and optimising final composite properties.

01 / Substrate preparation
Chip and sterilise
Chip or shred harvested stems to the desired particle size. Sterilise by autoclaving or pasteurisation to eliminate competing microorganisms before inoculation.
02 / Fungal selection
Match fungi to substrate
Pleurotus ostreatus colonises woody substrates fast and tolerates varied feedstocks. Trametes versicolor gives stronger binding on hardwood chips. Test compatibility with novel substrates first.
03 / Substrate blending
Mix for performance
Blend woody shrub chips with straw, reed, or other paludiculture biomass to balance structural support (from wood) and nutrition (from fibrous material). Typical ratios: 50-70% woody, 30-50% fibrous.
04 / Incubation
Control the environment
Maintain 25-30 C, 70-85% RH, in dark conditions with controlled CO2 exchange. Colonisation typically takes 10-25 days depending on species and substrate density.
05 / Post-processing
Press and dry
Inactivate growth by oven-drying at 60-80 C or hot-pressing. Hot-pressing densifies the composite and can raise flexural modulus substantially -- up to 34-80 MPa for trimitic species.
06 / End of life
Close the loop
All MBCs from paludiculture substrates are fully biodegradable and compostable. End-of-life material can be composted and returned to the land, completing the circular material cycle.
