Section 01

Introduction

Mycelium-based composites (MBCs) are an emerging class of sustainable biomaterials produced by cultivating fungal mycelium on lignocellulosic agricultural waste substrates. The growing mycelium binds substrate particles through its hyphal network, forming a solid, lightweight material that can be shaped into virtually any form before heat-inactivation terminates growth.

This report provides structured, scientifically grounded profiles for eight fungal species studied in MBC research, derived from peer-reviewed comparative studies and reviews published between 2019 and 2025.

Hyphal System Classification

The mechanical performance of MBCs is strongly linked to the hyphal system architecture of the fungal species. This is the single most important structural predictor of composite strength.

Trimitic — Generative + Skeletal + Binding hyphae → Superior strength Monomitic — Generative hyphae only → Lower strength, better insulation Dimitic — Generative + Skeletal or Binding → Intermediate

Post-Processing Effects

Hot-pressing (100–200 °C, 5–50 MPa) can raise flexural modulus from ~10–20 MPa to 34–80+ MPa. Drying to <10% moisture is essential for dimensional stability. Tanning improves water resistance for leather-like applications.

Common Substrates

All species thrive on agricultural and wood waste: straw, sawdust, hemp shives, corn husk, rice straw, coffee grounds, and cotton. Blended substrates often optimise colonisation speed and mechanical isotropy simultaneously.

Strain Profiles

Eight species profiled in approximate order of mechanical performance, from highest binding strength to lowest. Each card summarises growth requirements, texture, applications, and strength tier.

#1
Ganoderma williamsianum
No common name
Trimitic White-rot
Temperature25–30 °C
Humidity70–85%+
Incubation15–25 days
SubstrateSawdust, corn husk, rice straw

High-density mycelium with thick, hydrophobic hyphae and extremely low water absorption. First reported in MBC literature in 2022 (Aiduang et al.).

Applications
  • High-strength load-bearing construction panels
  • Dense structural composites and bricks
  • Durable packaging for heavy goods

Trimitic White-rot
Temperature25–30 °C

Incubation14–25 days

Very dense mycelial networks with a tough, compact structure. Strong surface layers with excellent homogeneous penetration through substrate volume.

#3
Trametes versicolor
Turkey Tail
Trimitic White-rot
Temperature25–30 °C
Humidity70–90%
Incubation7–21 days
SubstrateStraw, sawdust, hemp, cotton

Dense, fibrous network forming velvety or leathery surfaces under pressing. Among the fastest trimitic colonisers. Notable inherent fire resistance.

Applications
  • Acoustic panels and sound-absorbing insulation
  • Fire-resistant building panels
  • Leather-like sheets (post-processing)

#4
Ganoderma lucidum
Reishi · Lingzhi
Trimitic White-rot
Temperature25–30 °C
Humidity60–95%
Incubation15–30+ days
SubstrateHardwood sawdust, straw

Naturally hydrophobic; forms firm surface skins. Most extensively studied species (~25% of all MBC literature). Compressive strength 0.5–1.5 MPa.

Applications
  • Bricks and high-density construction
  • Mycelium leather and fabrics
  • Commercial MBC products (Ecovative, etc.)

#5
Fomes fomentarius
Hoof Fungus · Tinder Fungus · Amadou
Trimitic White-rot
Temperature~25 °C
Humidity70–85%
Incubation20–35 days
SubstrateHemp shives, hardwood

Very tough, woody, fibrous mycelium with high chitin content — mirrors the hard character of its fruiting body. Heat-pressing significantly boosts tensile strength.

Applications
  • Load-bearing structural composites
  • Leather-like sheet materials (amadou felt)
  • Impact-resistant engineering composites

#7
Pleurotus ostreatus
Oyster Mushroom
Monomitic White-rot
Temperature25–28 °C
Humidity75–90%
Incubation10–18 days
SubstrateStraw, sawdust, coffee, hemp

Softer, cottony texture; lower density. Lowest thermal conductivity of all common MBC species — ~0.032 W/(m·K) on wheat straw. Food-safe strain.

Applications
  • Thermal & acoustic insulation panels
  • Foam substitutes, lightweight packaging
  • Education and prototyping (ease of culture)

#8
Irpex lacteus
Milk-white Toothed Polypore
Dimitic White-rot
Temperature25–30 °C
Humidity70–85%
Growth rate8.0 mm/day (fastest)
SubstrateWood pulp, ag. waste blends

Fastest radial growth of all species (8.0 mm/day, Cristini et al. 2025). High chitin but weaker tensile performance due to dimitic architecture. Young's modulus 5–50 MPa.

Applications
  • Rapid inoculum production
  • General composites where speed > strength
  • Insulation boards, lightweight packaging

Standardised Growth Conditions

Across all species, the following incubation parameters are standard in MBC research. Deviations from these ranges generally slow colonisation and reduce composite homogeneity.

🌡
Temperature
25–30 °C
Most species have a broad tolerance; fruiting may require slight reduction
💧
Relative humidity
70–90%
Ganoderma species tolerate slightly lower RH without loss of performance
🌑
Light
Dark
All species prefer dark incubation; light inhibits mycelial extension in some strains
💨
Aeration / CO₂
Controlled
Good gas exchange increases density and colonisation rate in T. versicolor particularly
🌾
Substrate moisture
60–70%
At inoculation; final product dried to <10% for maximum mechanical performance
🔥
Inactivation
60–80 °C
Oven-drying or hot-pressing terminates growth; pressing simultaneously densifies the composite

References

Peer-reviewed sources forming the scientific basis of this report. All core papers are open-access or available via ResearchGate.

Core Papers

01 — Aiduang et al. (2022)
Mechanical, Physical, and Chemical Properties of Mycelium-Based Composites Produced from Various Lignocellulosic Residues and Fungal Species
Key source for G. williamsianum, L. sajor-caju; compressive/flexural/tensile/impact comparisons across sawdust, corn husk, and rice straw substrates.
→ ResearchGate
02 — Cristini et al. (2025)
A comparative study of mycelium films from nine fungal species for biocomposite applications — BioResources
Key source for D. confragosa tensile strength (~6.51 MPa), I. lacteus radial growth (8.0 mm/day), F. fomentarius properties, and chitin content vs. tensile properties.
→ BioResources Open Access
03 — Houette et al. (2022)
Growth and Mechanical Characterization of Mycelium-Based Composites — Biomimetics
Strong comparison of T. versicolor (trimitic) vs. P. ostreatus (monomitic); compressive strength and pressing/post-processing data.
→ MDPI Open Access
04 — Appels et al. (2019)
Fabrication factors influencing mechanical, moisture and water-related properties of mycelium-based composites — Composites Part A
Key reference for hot/cold pressing effects, density optimisation, and moisture management in Trametes and Pleurotus composites.
05 — Yang et al. (2021)
Material Function of Mycelium-Based Bio-Composite — Frontiers in Materials
Overview of species usage frequency, trimitic/monomitic hyphal system advantages, G. lucidum prevalence (~25% of MBC studies).
→ Frontiers Open Access

Supporting Sources

06 — Schmidt et al. (2023)
Mechanical, physical and thermal properties of composite materials produced with the basidiomycete Fomes fomentarius. Focus on construction potential and thermal performance.
07 — Jones et al. (2020)
Related works on Trametes and Ganoderma in engineering composites. Structural property benchmarking across substrates.
08 — Camilleri et al. (2025)
Updated comprehensive review of mycelium-based composites — species, substrates, post-processing, and applications.
09 — Sydor et al. (2022)
Review of fungi used in MBCs, including species lists and hyphal system classification for composite performance prediction.
10 — Schoder et al. (2024)
Environmental effects on mycelium growth rates — temperature, humidity, and CO₂ effects on colonisation dynamics across white-rot species.
