What is Mycelium

The Underground Internet of Nature

Mycelium is the vegetative part of fungi — a vast, thread-like network of hyphae that grows through soil, wood, and organic matter. Often invisible to the naked eye, it forms one of the most complex and intelligent networks found in nature.

A single teaspoon of soil can contain hundreds of metres of mycelial threads. These networks transport nutrients, communicate chemical signals between plants, and break down organic matter with remarkable efficiency.

What makes mycelium extraordinary for material science is its ability to self-assemble around substrate waste, creating composite structures with remarkable mechanical strength, thermal insulation, and acoustic dampening — all while remaining fully biodegradable.

Lab Journal

The Experiment

A detailed step-by-step account of the mycelium brick research — conducted independently as part of a Master's project. Real laboratory conditions, original images, real results.

01
Task One
Growing the First Bricks

The initial mycelium substrate was provided — a fine white web-like structure, soft and fuzzy like thick cotton, consistent with Pleurotus ostreatus (Oyster mushroom). This was used as the working assumption throughout.

Substrate was combined with 10% flour by total weight as a nutrient supplement, mixed thoroughly, and packed into perforated brick-shaped plastic moulds. Wrapped in plastic with ventilation holes, then placed in a Binder incubator at 23–24°C / 30–40% RH for 7–8 days.

👁️Visual: Pure white web-like structure; mycelium very soft and intact — consistent with oyster mushroom morphology.
🔬Microscope: Fine threads spread like a thick cotton layer — clear hyphal network confirmed.
✋Strength: Brick was strong with good flexibility before drying.
👃Smell: Distinct fresh mushroom odour — positive indicator of active mycelium growth.

After initial testing, bricks were heat-treated at 75–85°C for 3–4 hours. Post-heating: more rigid, mushroom smell replaced by dry wood scent with a faint fungal note. Significant moisture loss confirmed by weight.

02
Task Two
Culturing in Petri Dishes

Agar solution was prepared and sterilised in the Laborklav autoclave. After cooling, poured into 6 Petri dishes inside a laminar airflow chamber. Small mycelium samples from the brick surface were transferred onto each dish, sealed with parafilm, labelled, and incubated 7–8 days at 23–24°C.

✅4 of 6 dishes: Well-grown clean white mycelium colonies across the agar surface.
❌2 of 6 dishes: Green fungal contamination observed (likely Trichoderma sp.) — both discarded immediately.

The 4 successful cultures confirmed viable, transferable mycelium — a critical milestone for independent strain propagation.

Petri dish cultures — 8-day incubation on agar medium
Solid media plates prepared in laminar airflow chamber
03
Task Three
Cloning & Liquid Culture

To verify purity, sections from the 4 successful first-generation cultures were transferred to a second generation — on both solid agar media (6 new Petri dishes) and liquid culture slants (6 tubes).

The dual approach allowed cross-validation. After incubation, zero contamination was observed across all 12 samples — confirming a clean, pure mycelium culture ready for brick production.

✅Solid media (Petri): All 6 clean — consistent white hyphal growth pattern.
✅Liquid culture (slants): All 6 clean — mycelium visible as white floating mass in broth.
Liquid culture slants — 6 tubes, second-generation pure culture
Laborklav autoclave — used for media sterilisation
04
Task Four
New Waste Material Substrates

Using the self-cultivated mycelium, new bricks were grown incorporating two types of waste material (Type I and Type II). Both types had different particle sizes and physical composition.

📋Growth: Both waste material types colonised successfully by mycelium.
⚡Pre-heat: Noticeably more flexible and soft than original bricks — different material texture affected penetration.
🧱Post-heat: Harder than pre-heat, but showed brittle properties — broke along stress lines rather than flexing.
Waste material substrates — Type I (left) and Type II (right)
Initial mycelium strain provided — starting substrate
05
Task Five — Key Finding
Optimising Substrate Ratios

The critical experimental variable: varying the ratio of mycelium substrate to waste material to determine the optimal composite formulation. As waste concentration increased, mycelium penetration decreased — the fine texture reduced airflow essential for hyphal growth.

Ratio (Substrate : Waste)Structural ResultRating
60 : 40Excellent binding, rigid post-heat, fully intact structureExcellent
50 : 50Well formed, good integrity throughoutGood
40 : 60Slightly loose, brittleness increases, breaks under light forceFair
20 : 80No brick formation — mycelium unable to penetrate dense wasteFailed

Optimal formulation: 60% substrate : 40% waste material. Best balance of waste valorisation and structural integrity.

Following the brick experiments, mycelium paper was also successfully produced — opening a new product direction and confirming the breadth of applications from a single strain.

40:60 ratio — loose structure, increased brittleness

Equipment & Conditions

All experiments conducted in a university microbiology laboratory. Sterilisation via Laborklav autoclave. Incubation maintained in a Binder incubator. Post-growth heat treatment used a Thermo Scientific Heraeus oven.

About the Author

Biotechnologist · Chemical and Bioprocess engineer · Entrepreneur
"
Struggles are the building block of life — they enhance your personality, your way of thinking, and transform you into a new version of yourself.

I am a practical man, always driven by the urge to create something meaningful using my expertise. For a long time, a lack of opportunity created a void — lowering my confidence and making me question whether I was good enough. But each day of struggle taught me something, and with every lesson I kept pushing.

My vision is clear: take materials the world calls waste and transform them into something useful, sustainable, and meaningful to society. That conviction led me to mycelium — nature's hidden architect.

My journey began during my Master's curriculum, when Prof. Ingo Johannsen showed me a small mycelium brick prototype and asked: "Can you make something similar using waste materials?" I had never worked with fungi before. But I had a Bachelor's in Biotechnology, a hunger to prove myself, and a belief that struggle is not a dead end — it is a building block.

I started independently, without a manual or a mentor. What followed were real experiments, real data, and a real conviction that mycelium composites can reshape sustainable manufacturing. This website is the beginning of that story.
