## 2. The Experiment — Lab Journal

> **Note:** All experiments were conducted independently as part of a Master's project.
> Strain identity was not confirmed by DNA extraction due to project time constraints.
> Visual and microscopic observation was consistent with *Pleurotus ostreatus* (Oyster mushroom).

---

### Initial Strain Description

The provided mycelium substrate had the following characteristics:

- Fine white web-like surface, resembling frost or thick cotton
- Hyphae soft and fuzzy up close, spreading uniformly through the substrate
- Morphology and behaviour closely matching **Pleurotus ostreatus** (Oyster mushroom)
- **Working assumption throughout experiment:** Pleurotus ostreatus

---

### Overview of Experimental Tasks

| Task | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Task 1 | Grow initial bricks from provided substrate |
| Task 2 | Cultivate mycelium samples from bricks in Petri dishes |
| Task 3 | Clone cultures into solid media and liquid slants to verify purity |
| Task 4 | Grow new bricks using self-cultivated mycelium and two waste material types |
| Task 5 | Optimise substrate-to-waste ratio (50:50 / 60:40 / 40:60 / 20:80) |

### Task 1 — Growing the First Mycelium Bricks

**Objective:** Produce the initial brick using the provided mycelium substrate.

**Procedure:**

1. Take the provided substrate in a container
2. Add **10% flour by total weight** of substrate (used as a nutrient supplement)
3. Mix thoroughly to ensure even distribution
4. Pack mixture into **brick-shaped perforated plastic moulds**
5. Wrap mould completely in plastic and add ventilation holes
6. Place in **Binder incubator** at **23–24°C / 30–40% relative humidity**
7. Incubate for **7–8 days**

**Growth Conditions Used (based on Pleurotus ostreatus reference):**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Temperature | 23–24°C |
| Humidity | 30–40% RH |
| Duration | 7–8 days |
| Nutrient supplement | 10% flour by weight |

---

**Initial Observations (Pre-Heat Treatment):**

| Test | Observation |
|------|-------------|
| 👁️ Visual | Pure white web-like structure; mycelium soft and intact |
| 🔬 Microscope | Fine threads spread like thick cotton layer — clear hyphal network |
| ✋ Strength | Brick was strong and showed good flexibility |
| 👃 Smell | Distinct fresh mushroom odour |
| ⚖️ Moisture | Initial weight recorded for comparison |

---

**Heat Treatment:**
- Equipment: **Thermo Scientific Heraeus Oven**
- Temperature: **75–85°C**
- Duration: **3–4 hours**

**Post-Heat Treatment Observations:**

| Test | Observation |
|------|-------------|
| 👁️ Visual | Structure more intact and consolidated |
| 🔬 Microscope | Very tightly packed, strongly bound hyphal network |
| ✋ Strength | More rigid; slight flexibility remained |
| 👃 Smell | Mushroom odour mostly gone — replaced by dry wood with faint fungal note |
| ⚖️ Moisture | Bricks noticeably lighter — significant moisture loss confirmed |

---

### Task 2 — Culturing Mycelium in Petri Dishes

**Objective:** Transfer mycelium from brick surface to agar plates to establish a pure culture.

**Procedure:**

1. Prepare agar solution
2. Sterilise in **Laborklav autoclave**
3. Cool and pour into **6 Petri dishes** inside a laminar airflow chamber
4. Allow to solidify
5. Transfer small mycelium samples from brick surface onto each dish
6. Seal with **parafilm**, label with date and number
7. Incubate for **7–8 days** at 23–24°C / 30–40% RH

**Results:**

| Dish | Result |
|------|--------|
| 4 of 6 | ✅ Well-grown white mycelium colonies |
| 2 of 6 | ❌ Contamination — green fungal colony observed → **discarded** |

> **Note:** Green colony is likely a *Trichoderma* species — a common lab contaminant. The 4 clean dishes were carried forward.

---

### Task 3 — Cloning into Solid Media and Liquid Culture

**Objective:** Verify purity of established culture by creating second-generation clones across two media types.

**Procedure:**

From the 4 successful first-generation Petri dishes:

- Prepare **6 new Petri dishes** (solid agar media) — second generation
- Prepare **6 liquid culture slants** — second generation
- Incubate under same conditions
- Observe for contamination

**Results:**

| Medium | Dishes | Result |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Solid (Petri) | 6 of 6 | ✅ Clean mycelium growth — no contamination |
| Liquid (slants) | 6 of 6 | ✅ Clean mycelium growth — no contamination |

> **Conclusion:** The culture was confirmed as pure mycelium. No contamination in any second-generation sample across either media type. Confident to proceed to brick production using self-cultivated strain.

---

### Task 4 — Bricks from Self-Cultivated Strain + New Waste Materials

**Objective:** Test whether self-cultivated mycelium (from solid and liquid cultures) could grow on two new types of waste material.

**Setup:**
- Mycelium inoculum sourced from both solid and liquid second-generation cultures
- Two distinct waste material types provided (labelled Type I and Type II)
- Brick preparation followed same procedure as Task 1

**Results:**

| Aspect | Observation |
|--------|-------------|
| Growth | ✅ Both waste materials colonised successfully |
| Pre-heat | More flexible and softer than original bricks |
| Post-heat | Harder but **brittle** — broke along stress lines rather than flexing |

> **Interpretation:** The different physical properties of the waste materials (finer particle size, denser packing) affected how deeply and uniformly the mycelium could penetrate, resulting in less structural cohesion.

---

### Task 5 — Optimising Substrate-to-Waste Ratio

**Objective:** Find the optimal ratio of mycelium substrate to waste material for maximum structural integrity.

**Ratios Tested:** 60:40 / 50:50 / 40:60 / 20:80 (substrate : waste material)

**Results:**

| Ratio (Substrate : Waste) | Structural Quality | Rating |
|---------------------------|-------------------|--------|
| **60 : 40** | Excellent — strong binding, rigid post-heat, intact structure | ⭐ Excellent |
| 50 : 50 | Well formed, good integrity throughout | ✅ Good |
| 40 : 60 | Slightly loose, increased brittleness, breaks under light force | ⚠️ Fair |
| 20 : 80 | No brick formation — mycelium could not penetrate dense waste | ❌ Failed |

**Key Finding:**

> As waste material percentage increased, mycelium's ability to penetrate the substrate decreased. The primary cause: the **fine texture of the waste material reduced airflow** through the mixture, and mycelium requires adequate oxygen to grow deeply into the substrate matrix.
>
> **Optimal formulation: 60% mycelium substrate : 40% waste material.**
> This ratio provides the best balance of waste valorisation efficiency and structural integrity.
